Stroke

What is a Stroke?

"Stroke" is a word that people sometimes use to describe something that can be pretty good (like stroking your pet's fur, or a stroke of luck - those are pretty nice things!) or something that can be pretty bad. When we talk about stroke on this website, we're talking about something pretty bad: stroke is a serious health problem that can affect a lot of people if they're not careful.

Our bodies need a steady supply of blood for them to work properly. We don't normally have to worry about this - our hearts are very good at making sure that our blood goes where it needs to go! This blood carries oxygen and all sorts of nutrients that our bodies need to work, and is pumped through very thin tubes in our bodies called blood vessels, or arteries and veins. Without it, the cells in our bodies won't work!

When some parts of our brain don't get the blood they need to function, they might become damaged and might stop working altogether. This is what we call a stroke. Strokes usually occur because there's a blockage that stops blood from getting to the brain - and sometimes, these blockages can cause blood vessels to burst and cause other problems. We also call this a stroke, because the reasons are very similar.

Types of Stroke

The most common type of stroke is when blood can't go where it's supposed to because a blood vessel leading to the brain has become blocked. This usually happens because of a blood clot, and it's nasty. The big, fancy word for this is ischemic stroke.

Sometimes, brain cells are destroyed because of a weak or thin blood vessel bursting, which causes blood to spill out. This is called hemorrhagic (hem-oh-reh-jik) stroke and happen more often when the walls of vessels have been weakened by high blood pressure.
